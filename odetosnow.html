Ode to Snow

March 23, 2020

You, snow, always glistening,
brave like the wounded sergeant
who knows his days are numbered,
yet still fights for his last breath.
Always pure, a product of Paradise.
You descend from the holy clouds above,
blessing the bare ground with your presence.

You are a delicate dancer with graceful limbs,
able to morph into thousands of shapes.
You are brilliantly white, slender and satisfying,
an arctic fox gracefully cantering throughout its home.

I want to stare at your blissfulness, your simplicity,
for all of eternity.
I want to forever hear you
crunching under my feet, padding the ground beneath me.
I want to feel your frigid flakes cascading through my outstretched fingers,
your icy crystals decorating the surface of my tongue.
Yet you are capricious, never unwavering,
eager to regress to a primordial state.
Your intrinsic impermanence only makes you more significant,
an ephemeral escape from life’s adversity.

And like all good things do,
your sojourn must end.
As the troops of flowers and armies of green
slowly make their advance,
I must bid a sorrow farewell
to the glistening, pure, delicate soldier
who seals his days
with a final view
of the light.

Note: This ode is not only a metaphor for the impermanence that characterizes today’s society, but it is also the story of my first winter in a new state as a twelve-year-old. I saw snow for the first time during the winter of 2016, the year that my sister had left to college and my family had moved from Saratoga to Seattle. I remember the ephemeral joy I felt staring at the glistening white powder barely covering the grass. I missed home. I missed my sister. I missed my friends. Yet, this trivial act of nature reminded me of the beauty that could be found in a new place, of hope, of happiness—even if it only lasted for a few days. I witnessed the beauty in transience for the first time—that feeling of unstifled love and appreciation for something that is infinite in its power but inevitably temporary. This poem shares that feeling.
