The Park

March 15, 2019

In the summer, the park is flooded with kids, climbing up the slides and playing tag on the grass. There are a few picnic tables at the back, where the moms chat about schools and how Ebony’s daughter was off to college, about recipes for lemon cake and pavlova. The dads are out on the field playing a friendly game of golf, although it does get a bit competitive when Tyrone hits a birdie and Jamal tries to hit an eagle but ends up with a bogey instead.

In the fall, the park is empty, except for the lone kid reading the newest school book on the lawn. A few moms and dads still come by every week to continue repairing the community garden, but the majority consider it a lost cause. On Saturday it smells of grass clippings and eucalyptus leaves, because the city groundkeepers finish their weekly cleanup routine. But most of the time all you can see are the tree branches waving in the August wind.

In the winter, the seesaw is covered with snow, and the kids spend their holidays sledding down the fields past the picnic tables or building igloos under the slides. The parents stand, shivering, and drink hot cocoa while planning Christmas shopping and New Year’s celebrations. On Thursdays, Ol’ Daddy Darryl—Jalen’s grandpa—brings his axe and a harmonica, and when he’s not searching for the perfect holiday tree he’s playing “Row Your Boat” and “Early Morning Rain” for the kids and the occasional parent or two.

In the spring, sometimes I sit on the swing and watch the dandelions shiver or the bluebirds fly past the fir trees. The kids are at home, prepping for their end-of-year exams, and their parents are bickering about where to go for summer vacation. So I pretty much get the park to myself—a nice change from the usual bustle. But it won’t be like this for long. It’s been a month since the recession hit, and most families have already been forced out by high prices and what the realtors call “gentrification”—in other words, doomsday for us folk. But it’s okay. I’m sure we’ll end up in a place with a park just like this one. A park with eucalyptus leaves on autumn Sundays. A park with golfing competitions and endless chatter. A park that brings us all together like Darryl’s harmonica on a bitter winter evening.
