Credit Card

July 11, 2019

I still remember how, long ago, we used to be the good guys. My mom led a SWAT team, and my dad was one of the officers; my older brother Tommy was in school, studying to be a neuroscientist; and I was entering my first year of high school. That was long before our bedtime stories turned into sirens and we stashed money in our backpacks instead of textbooks and pencils. When my mom passed away during one of the missions, my dad quit the task force, and Tommy dropped out of Austin to get a job working at the local restaurant to pay off our mortgage. But that didn’t work out, and the next thing I knew, he was fired and I became a high school dropout. Our car got repossessed, we lost our house, and it’s been a few years since we’ve seen a credit card.

Every day, Tommy and I find a new mom-and-pop shop downtown, and I slip in, wrapped in my hoodie, and get out with a loaf of bread or some potato chips so that the three of us can make it through the day. Every day, I try not to think about the old lives we used to lead. I mean, we’re the bad guys now, and nothing can change that. Because food is more important than integrity. Because our values were repossessed along with our car. We have no choice. At least, that’s what we tell ourselves.
