A Life of Computation

Inspired by Malba Tahan's The Man Who Counted
February 06, 2019



At dawn’s first light, great Beremiz Samir,
hallowed calculator, was urged by the
deathless Allah to mount his velvet sandals
and withdraw from his humble home to meet
the astonishing sight before his eyes.
Royal Prince Cluzir Shah,
his golden velvet cloak and white turban
shining brighter than the sharp scimitars
that donned the waists of his thousand servants,
stopped before great Beremiz and told him:
“A poor wise man is he who seeks out the rich,
but a nobler rich man is he who seeks
wise men. As the foremost prince of Baghdad
I would be honored if you became the
secretary to my wealthy estate.”

After a friendly salaam and prayer,
to the prince’s surprise Samir replied:
“O my generous prince!
I unfortunately cannot accept
the benevolent offer you suggest.
I have been pining for the ability
to reach my beloved home in India,
and the sheik offered me a quick voyage
if I gave lessons to his daughter Telassim.”

The prince merely smiled and proposed a plan:
“I can give you faster travel if you teach me
the riddle of the rajah’s pearls.”

With skill matching that of Allah himself,
the wise Beremiz launched out on his tail:
“Prince Cluzir, shining among your subjects,
I would be honored to recite the tale
in exchange for a trip to India.
I must arrive home with the utmost speed
and reclaim the household waiting for me,
so I will proceed with an abridged tale—
a dying rajah left his six daughters
thirty-six pearls to be divided up
such that the eldest daughter received one
pearl and a seventh of those remaining,
the second eldest received two and a
seventh of those remaining, and so on.
Thinking that the division was unfair,
the youngest daughters called a wise old judge
who explained the ingenious clarity
of the division.

The first daughter received
one plus a seventh of thirty-five, five.
The second received two plus a seventh
of twenty-eight, or four, which sums to six.
Thus the judge handed out pearls equally
until none remained and the daughters left.”

The prince promised Samir a rapid trip,
but when dawn’s first light shone from Islam’s skies
Samir caught sight of the ill-tempered sheik
Tara Tir among cruel assassins.
Angered by the noble reputation
gained by Samir along his famous quest,
the sheik was plotting a violent ambush
which led the prince to abandon Samir
from fear of his life.
After Samir’s companion from Baghdad,
Hanak Tade Maia, caught sight of the
angered sheik Tara Tir, the great Allah
brought Captain Hassan Maurique before Samir.
Muscles rippling, the great Maurique approached Samir
and requested, “O great work of Allah,
crafted by the Prophet, Beremiz Samir:
please gift me with the tale of the camels
and how you ended a violent quarrel.
For this I will grant you any favor
I can obtain with my two weakened hands.”

So Samir launched out on his tale with ease:
“I am flattered by your graciousness,
royal Maurique—your words are as down deep
inside your heart as those of great Allah.
Of course I will tell you the tale of the camels.
Near the start of my India-bound journey,
I did clearly hear wild and fierce insults
erupting from the midst of an old inn.
When I asked why the men were quarrelling,
the oldest immediately explained,
‘We three received an inheritance of
thirty-five camels. A half goes to me,
a third goes to my brother Hamed and
my youngest brother Harim takes a ninth.
But these fractions yield no precise numbers,
so how will we divide up our camels?’”
With a plan to speed his and his friend’s journey,
Samir offered the brothers the camel
of Hanak in order to end the quarrel.
Samir gestured to the oldest brother:
“Now, you receive a half of thirty-six,
eighteen. Hamed receives twelve and Harim
takes four, which sums to thirty-four, leaving
two remaining and giving you each more
than you would receive with thirty-five total.
Hanak will take back his camel and the
last one I will take to speed our journey
as a present for ending your quarrel.”

Royal Maurique, head of the sultan’s guard,
burst into applause and replied with kind words:
“I had never thought that mathematics
was as prodigious as you described.
Let me know any favor I can grant,
and it shall be granted.”
                                            As Beremiz
Began to respond, Hanak interjected,
“We would be extremely grateful if you
could prevent the ambush from Tara Tir.
He harbors resentment against Samir’s
skill, and has long been spying on us.”
Maurique bowed before the great Beremiz”
“I could not have imagined that a man
like Tara Tir could disturb the life of
a prized scholar like great Samir himself!
By the Prophet, I will put an end to
this matter at once.”

Thanking Maurique for his loyal service,
Hanak quickly ushered Samir away
in order to check his friend’s fighting spirit.
Samir exclaimed, “I formed a logical plan!”
I can defeat the young, cowardly sheik
hiding behind the wrath of tougher men.
If I challenge him to a brief contest
testing both our geometrical skills,
I can triumph and continue the trip.”

Hanak replied in turn, “O Beremiz,
your talents lie in calculations,
not facing a group of trained assassins.
No need to risk your young and healthy life
just to show your mathematical skills.”

Soon after they departed from Maurique,
Hanak noticed wise Samir’s wistful gaze:
“What is this? Some longing for your country?
Or simply planning new calculations?”

“My friend of Baghdad,” Beremiz replied,
“Nostalgia and calculations are not
completely unrelated. Everything
in my world is dominated by the
calculations required throughout one’s life.”

Upon reaching the final caliph’s home,
Samir stood gazing up at the palace,
a match for even the mansion above
where deathless Allah watches over men.
Samir was given an arduous challenge:
to receive any prize that he asked for,
he would have to compete before a crowd
with four foreign mathematicians.
After entering the holy palace
and reciting a prayer from the Koran,
Beremiz heard the caliph mutter
an insult to his skilled calculations.
Anger building up within his breast,
understanding that to reach India
and find his true love with utmost speed
he must accept the challenge and perhaps
suffer defeat before the world’s wise men,
Samir still agreed, with Allah’s support,
to hear the first mathematics challenge.

Wise Abul Hassan Ali stepped forward:
“Knowledge must observe the facts and then, from them,
deduce its laws. But how do we arrive
at the truth? Here is my question for you—
is it possible to arrive at a
false deduction from only truth?”
                                                            Samir
thought carefully and then explained with tact,
“Let us suppose a mathematician
wished to find square roots of four-figure sums.
Picking two thousand twenty-five, three thousand
twenty-five, and nine thousand eighty-one,
he would find the answers of forty-five,
fifty-five and ninety-nine that all
equate to the sums of the two numbers
obtained from dividing the original
four-figure sum after the second digit.
Therefore the calculator can falsely
deduce that any square root can be found
with this method, and hence is using truth
to make an erroneous assumption.”
Amazed at the reply, Abul Hassan
was replaced by the sage Nascif Rahal.

As the scholar approached, Samir’s thoughts whirred,
his mind blank of all but calculations.
Even amidst the risk of losing a
rapid journey to his home in India,
Samir thought calmly and rationally,
preparing himself to employ all his
accumulated mathematics skill.

Yet Rahal asked a surprising question
that forced Samir to rely on quick wit:
“Knowledge alone does not make a man
wise—imagination is required.
I would like Beremiz Samir to tell
a fable in which division of three
by three is suggested but not carried
out, and division of three by two is
carried out with a remainder of zero.”

As Samir launched out on his clever tale,
Allah increased the volume of his voice
and painted his words with a sense of awe:
“A lion, a tiger, and a jackal
had found a sheep, a pig, and a rabbit
unaware of any predators.
The tiger suggested they divide the prey
such that the lion received the whole sheep,
the tiger received the pig and finally,
the jackal received the small rabbit.
The lion killed the tiger and asked the jackal
to divide the three different animals
more fairly among the two of them.
The jackal gave all three delicious meals
to the lion, hence dividing the prey
among the two without a remainder.
Thus dividing three by three was proposed
while three by two without a remainder
was carried out by the clever jackal.”

Next, the third sage Macudo asked Samir:
“Out of three white disks and two black disks
that are identical in all but color,
a random disk is strapped to the back
of each of three princes. The first king’s son
can see the disks of his fellow princes
but still does not determine the answer.
The second prince views the disk of the third
but cannot calculate the right answer.
Finally the third prince correctly figures
the color of the disk upon his back.
Here is my question—how does the third prince
determine that his disk is colored white?”

Motivated by his desire to reach home
and find his true lover at last,
Samir responded with apparent ease:
“The first prince erred because he was unsure,
suggesting that the next two princes’ disks
were either both white or alternating.
If both were the color white the third prince
must have been wearing black. But if one disk
was black and the other white, the third prince
must have been wearing white in order for
the second prince to still make a mistake.”

As applause was showered upon Samir,
The fourth sage Mohildin Banabixacar
whose name was known to all under the sky
gave Beremiz the ultimate puzzle:
“A merchant obtains eight identical
pearls, with one weighing slightly less than
the others. How can he determine which
pearl is the lightest with only two weighings
and no weights?”
                                 The wise Samir soon replied,
“Simply divide the pearls into three groups,
where two groups contain three pearls and the third
has the remaining two. Weigh the first two groups
and if they are the same weight we know that
the lightest pearl is in the third group. Hence
we can weigh those two to find the answer.
If the first two groups weigh differently,
then weigh two pearls from the lighter group
to find the pearl that is the lightest.”

As the crowd watched foreign Samir with awe,
the mathematician began his speech,
his words crafted by great Allah himself:
“O people of this generous caliph,
I have faced all the problems with success.
Each wise man has only trembled before
the calculations whirring in my mind.
All foes have vanquished before my prodigy.
My wisdom and soul have attained triumph—
now lord, grant the gift you promised to me.”

The caliph praised him for his flawless skill
and offered him twenty-thousand dinars
and a government career in Baghdad.
Yet Beremiz refused the great rewards,
instead asking, along with a swift trip
to India, for a surprising gift:
“Give me the hand of the woman I love—
the shining beauty, dear young Telassim.”

The caliph readily granted his wish:
“O Beremiz Samir, your supreme skill
has struck me and Allah himself with awe.
With no doubt I will speed you on your way
with thirty-five of my finest horsemen
and the hand of your love, dear Telassim.
I and all four mathematicians here
will also shower you with lustrous gifts
to escort you under Islam’s blue skies.”
Samir in turn responded tactfully,
“My dear caliph, to you I owe my life.
Thank you, great lord, for gifting me with the
opportunity to finally reach
my long-missed India.”
